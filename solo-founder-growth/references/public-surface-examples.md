# Public Surface Examples

Use this when reviewing a landing page, pricing page, homepage, demo, or public product surface. Keep it compact: these are patterns to apply, not a swipe file to copy.

## Core Rule

Start with the buyer's outcome, show the artifact fast, then make the buying path match the cost structure.

Do not lead with model names, frameworks, architecture, or internal complexity unless the buyer is explicitly technical and that detail changes trust or purchase intent.

## Buyer Choice

Pick easier buyers first:

- Prosumers, founders, SMBs, agencies, and practitioners with card authority.
- Buyers who can try without legal, procurement, IT, or security review.
- Buyers replacing a known expensive/manual alternative.
- Buyers who can see the output or value quickly.

Avoid starting with legal/procurement/security-heavy buyers unless strong pull already exists. TypingMind and Chatbase now have serious team/security surfaces, but their original wedges were self-serve and immediately useful.

## Outcome-First Framing

Good product surfaces from the researched founders lead with outcomes:

- ShipFast: launch your startup in days, not weeks.
- HeadshotPro: professional headshots for much less than a physical photo shoot.
- DataFast: find marketing channels that drive paying customers, not pageviews.
- Chatbase: AI customer support agents for customer experience.
- Bannerbear: automate marketing asset generation.
- TypingMind: own a private multi-model AI workspace.

If the headline could only excite an engineer, rewrite it around the buyer's job.

## Show The Artifact Fast

Show the thing the buyer gets:

- Headshots, interiors, generated media, screenshots, templates, dashboards, API demos, reports, examples, or before/after states.
- If the product is an API, show generated outputs and integration examples.
- If the product is analytics, show the decision it enables, not only charts.
- If the product is a workflow tool, show the completed workflow and result.

The buyer should understand the value before reading feature lists.

## Pricing Model Fit

Match pricing to cost structure:

- One-time: templates, courses, local apps, BYO-key tools, finite output packages, reports.
- Credits/usage: generated media, API calls, AI messages, exports, jobs, documents.
- Subscription: hosted software, sync, teams, analytics, customer support automation, ongoing business value.
- Enterprise/custom: only after self-serve pull or real deal friction.

Do not price low when support, reliability, or variable costs are high. Low-quality users can consume the founder's week.

## Proof And Objections

Useful proof patterns:

- Customer count, usage count, generated assets, revenue proof, reviews, trusted logos, or named testimonials.
- Free trial, free tool, demo, refund, or clear paid boundary.
- FAQ that answers ownership, refund, billing, API costs, credits, privacy, data handling, and cancellation.

Trust/compliance is valid when the product touches business data, support workflows, or teams. It should not become early-stage avoidance unless buyers are already asking for it.

## Review Checklist

- `Buyer`: who can buy without a committee?
- `Outcome`: what concrete result do they get?
- `Artifact`: what is shown above the fold or within the first screen?
- `Alternative`: what expensive/manual/status-quo option does this replace?
- `CTA`: can the buyer try, buy, upload, generate, or book now?
- `Pricing`: does the model match cost and value?
- `Proof`: what evidence says this already works?
- `Friction`: what legal, security, data, procurement, or support burden blocks self-serve?
- `Cut`: what technical explanation can be removed or pushed lower?
