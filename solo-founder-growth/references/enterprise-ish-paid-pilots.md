# Enterprise-Ish Paid Pilots

Use this when a serious organization wants more than the standard product: founder help, internal data, security answers, procurement, custom workflow setup, private deployment, integrations, roadmap priority, or support obligations.

The point is not to become an enterprise-sales agent. The point is to stop a solo founder from selling unlimited founder time for a cheap SaaS plan.

## Route By Friction, Not Company Size

A large company can stay self-serve if it uses the standard product under standard terms without founder help.

A small regulated company becomes enterprise-ish when it asks for:

- custom legal terms, DPA redlines, procurement setup, PO/manual invoice, or vendor onboarding;
- security questionnaires, custom assurances, trust packets, or data-flow reviews;
- proprietary or sensitive data evaluation beyond the standard product boundary;
- private cloud, self-hosting, air-gap, SSO, RBAC, audit logs, SLA, or custom deployment;
- custom integrations, connectors, migration, data preparation, or organization-specific configuration;
- roadmap priority, exclusivity, special support, or ongoing founder availability.

Rule: a cheap plan buys the standard product, standard terms, and standard support. It never buys procurement work, custom security assurances, private deployment, implementation, roadmap priority, or founder availability.

Do not use looser access to rescue weak buyer proof. Unlimited calls, broad custom help, unclear refund promises, or "we will figure it out together" language usually increases expectations faster than it increases trust.

## Offer Selector

Use the smallest offer that matches the friction:

- `Free trial`: standard product, standard data boundary, self-directed evaluation, no custom terms or setup.
- `Free research partner`: interviews, workflow observation, redacted examples, or prototype feedback. No production use, custom delivery, or roadmap commitment.
- `Paid pilot`: one defined workflow, mostly standard product, real users or representative data, measurable result, plausible rollout.
- `Design partner`: representative buyer with urgency and implementation capability who gives structured feedback and may influence roadmap. Paid by default when real data, useful output, or founder build work is involved.
- `Paid technical discovery`: private deployment, air-gap, unusual security architecture, proprietary-data feasibility, or unclear implementation path. Deliver architecture, data-flow, risks, supported configuration, and quote.
- `Enterprise quote`: custom legal, procurement, SSO/RBAC, SLA, security review, private deployment, or roadmap commitment.
- `Implementation SOW`: corpus prep, connector setup, migration, private deployment, training, organization configuration, or customer-specific evaluation harness.
- `Decline or pause`: no champion, no budget owner, no data, no decision date, no post-pilot budget, or demands for unlimited/custom liability.

## Reciprocal Buyer Proof

Before founder-assisted work starts, require:

- named champion;
- economic buyer or budget owner identified;
- one concrete workflow;
- representative test data or approved data substitute;
- qualified reviewer who can judge output quality;
- procurement/security owner when those processes are involved;
- written success criteria;
- decision date and decision meeting scheduled;
- plausible post-pilot budget or annual price range;
- customer-side tasks and owners.

If the buyer will not commit time, data, ownership, or budget, do not compensate by committing more founder time.

## Paid Pilot One-Pager

Keep each pilot small and written down:

- `Goal`: one question the pilot must answer.
- `Scope`: one workflow, one bounded corpus, one supported deployment profile.
- `Users`: named users, usually up to five at first.
- `Duration`: six weeks by default; eight weeks hard maximum.
- `Start condition`: signed scope and cleared payment or non-cancellable PO.
- `Buyer inputs`: champion, reviewer, data/access, weekly attendance, internal owner.
- `Success criteria`: 1-3 measurable criteria agreed before kickoff.
- `Data boundary`: allowed data, model/provider mode, telemetry/support data, retention/deletion, verification responsibility.
- `Support`: kickoff, one weekly 45-minute session, final review, one async channel.
- `Founder-time cap`: default 15-20 hours unless the fee explicitly supports more.
- `Deliverables`: configured environment, brief training, evaluation results, documented gaps, rollout recommendation.
- `Exclusions`: custom connectors, certification, legal/compliance conclusions, source code, unlimited changes, 24/7 support, production SLA.
- `Conversion`: post-pilot package, annual price range, decision participants, decision date.

At the deadline, the pilot becomes the pre-agreed organizational package or returns to self-serve scope. Do not silently convert a successful high-touch pilot to a cheap public plan.

## Pricing And Economics

These are starting-point floors for trust-heavy solo/tiny-team products, not universal market truth:

- Founder-assisted pilot: at least `10k`.
- Internal/sensitive-data or custom-evaluation pilot: often `15k-25k`.
- Private-deployment technical discovery: often `10k-20k`.
- Enterprise annual floor once procurement/security/custom legal starts: roughly `30k+`.
- Private-cloud or air-gapped annual relationship: likely `60k+` plus implementation, or decline.

Private rejection-floor formula:

```text
pilot floor = max(
  10k,
  20% of plausible first-year contract value,
  2x fully loaded delivery cost
)
```

Quote externally on business value and scope, not founder hours. Use delivery cost only as the private check that prevents underpricing.

Separate:

- recurring software;
- implementation or onboarding;
- bespoke development;
- private deployment/support;
- compliance or security work.

Pilot revenue is validation/services revenue, not MRR. Implementation fees are not ARR. A single institutional pilot is not proof of an enterprise segment.

## Custom-Work Classifier

Classify every request before agreeing:

1. `Standard configuration`: already supported; included only if inside written pilot scope.
2. `Reusable product capability`: useful to the broader ICP; apply normal evidence gates before building.
3. `Customer-specific implementation`: connector, migration, deployment, data prep, report format, or workflow configuration; separate SOW, price, and acceptance criteria.
4. `Customer-specific operating responsibility`: regulatory interpretation, bespoke compliance consulting, 24/7 operations, unique infrastructure, certification responsibility, or open-ended support; decline or involve a specialist partner.

Manual-first does not mean free-first. A buyer may fund customer-specific work, but it does not silently redefine the core product.

## Trust And Security Boundary

For trust-heavy products, maintain an approved facts packet:

- standard data-flow descriptions by mode;
- model providers, subprocessors, retention, deletion, telemetry, support access, and update mechanism;
- supported deployment profiles;
- known limitations and unsupported claims;
- human-review responsibility;
- security controls already true today;
- roadmap controls clearly labeled as not yet available.

Agents may draft answers from this packet, but the founder must approve every security, privacy, legal, and regulatory statement. Unknown answers remain unknown; do not invent attestations.

Compliance is feasibility first. Identify likely blockers early, but do full certification only after qualified demand or existing obligation.

## Stop Rules

Pause or say no when:

- no budget owner or economic buyer appears;
- no decision date or closure process exists;
- no representative data or reviewer is available;
- the buyer repeatedly misses agreed actions;
- the pilot scope keeps expanding;
- the buyer asks for free production use;
- custom legal/security/procurement work appears before commercial range is accepted;
- the buyer wants unlimited support, SLA, liability, exclusivity, IP transfer, or roadmap veto;
- private deployment or air-gap is requested before value proof;
- the logo/reference value is the main compensation.
- the buyer treats the pilot as open-ended consulting rather than a scoped evaluation.

Count logo value as zero unless the contract grants usable reference or case-study rights. Even then, reference rights do not replace acceptable cash economics.

## AI Delegation

Agents can help with:

- account and stakeholder research;
- call preparation and transcript summaries;
- CRM updates and follow-up drafts;
- pilot SOW drafts from approved templates;
- request classification;
- security-questionnaire extraction;
- draft answers from an approved facts packet;
- contract redline risk summaries;
- evaluation datasets and result summaries;
- weekly pilot reports;
- support triage and reproduction steps.

Founder must own:

- qualification and whether to pursue the account;
- live relationship with champion and economic buyer;
- price, discounts, scope, and negotiation;
- every customer-visible promise;
- legal acceptance, liability, indemnity, IP terms, and security attestations;
- regulatory claims and safety/compliance boundaries;
- final roadmap and custom-work decisions.

Rule: agents may retrieve, compare, summarize, and draft. They may not attest.

## Evidence Notes

- Heavybit recommends paid pilots/POCs, short timeframes, clear goals, and shared customer responsibility.
- B2B pilot guidance repeatedly warns against free pilots when the customer expects founder work, setup, or guided onboarding.
- Enterprise pilot frameworks warn that undefined success criteria, no decision owner, scope creep, and no closure process create pilot purgatory.
- Design-partner guidance is mixed on pricing, but consistent on selecting representative, urgent, capable partners and documenting expectations.
- Founder-led sales remains useful because serious B2B conversations are also market research, objection discovery, and trust building.
