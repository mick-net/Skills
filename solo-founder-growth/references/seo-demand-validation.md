# SEO Demand Validation

Use this when the bottleneck is search demand, SEO, public-surface traffic, content gaps, free tools, pSEO, or backlink authority. Do not load it by default for every founder coaching request.

Core principle: search data is a demand signal and distribution map. It is not payment proof. Use it to find existing demand, choose the right public asset, and avoid building where no one is looking.

This reference provides routers and guardrails, not a full SEO course. Prefer buyer proof, clear public surface, and small paid/message tests before compounding SEO builds.

## When To Use

Use for:
- Prelaunch ideas that may be SEO-led.
- Launched products with no traffic.
- Public pages that do not match buyer/searcher intent.
- Deciding whether to build a free tool, comparison page, integration page, or content hub.
- `$1k+ MRR` products looking for repeatable organic acquisition.

Do not use for:
- Hidden backend work.
- Generic blog planning before buyer/proof is clear.
- Dashboard projects before the next MRR question is known.
- Broad pSEO before payment, a real data source, or clear long-tail demand.

## Search-Demand Gate

Before recommending SEO-led build work, answer:

- `Buyer/searcher`: who is searching, and are they the buyer or only a researcher?
- `Painful job`: what job, output, alternative, or problem are they searching for?
- `Search demand`: are there real searches across the main keyword and related terms?
- `Commercial value`: is there CPC, paid competition, purchase intent, or a believable paid offer?
- `Competition`: can a small site plausibly compete on authority, page quality, or niche specificity?
- `Intent`: what page type already ranks?
- `Ability to exceed`: can the founder create something meaningfully better this week?
- `Paid boundary`: where does the asset lead to payment, trial, setup, pilot, or email capture?

If search demand is weak and there is no reachable community/channel, route back to validation instead of building.

## SERP-As-Brief

Treat the current search result page as the product/content brief, not as a decoration after the idea is chosen.

For each target query:

1. Inspect what already ranks: tool, guide, comparison, directory, template, calculator, video, Reddit/forum thread, marketplace, or product page.
2. Identify the searcher's expected outcome: answer, shortlist, generated output, benchmark, quote, file, checklist, booking, or purchase.
3. Choose the smallest asset that matches that intent.
4. Add the paid boundary before building: signup, trial, checkout, paid audit, paid pilot, booked demo, or email capture.
5. If the SERP shows tools and the founder proposes a blog post, challenge it. If it shows comparisons and the founder proposes a generic feature page, challenge it. If it shows forums, extract pain language and validate in that community before building.

The solo-founder version is not "publish more." It is "build the asset searchers already expect, with a conversion path."

## Search Signal Vs Proof

Use search and ads data to understand language, intent, demand, and distribution. Do not treat them as proof of payment.

- `Keyword tools`: validate query existence, wording, rough demand, trend, CPC, paid competition, and SERP shape.
- `Search Console`: validates how the founder's existing public surface appears in Google: queries, impressions, clicks, indexed pages, and page/query mismatch.
- `Small Google Ads test`: can validate promise/query/CTA fit quickly.
- `Analytics and Stripe`: validate signup, activation, payment, repeat payment, and retention.

Search volume can justify a landing page, free tool, manual offer, or small content batch. It cannot justify a multi-week product build without buyer contact, paid proof, or strong existing-customer evidence.

## AI-Search Visibility Gate

Use AI-search visibility as a perception and source-selection signal, not proof of demand or payment.

Before recommending AI-search work, answer:

- `Prompt/job`: what buyer question should surface this product, page, or source?
- `Current answer`: what do Google AI Overviews/AI Mode, ChatGPT search, Gemini, Claude, or Perplexity currently recommend or cite?
- `Source gap`: is the product missing because the public page is not crawlable, credible, specific, useful, or mentioned by real users/customers?
- `Buyer relevance`: would being cited here reach likely buyers, or only researchers and non-buyers?
- `Sourceable asset`: what page would deserve citation: comparison, benchmark, original data, docs, example, template, case study, integration page, or free tool?
- `Truth constraint`: can the founder improve the page without fake claims, fake reviews, fake community posts, or AI slop?
- `Conversion path`: what should a qualified visitor do next?

Do not recommend AI-search hacks. For Google, normal SEO, indexability, helpful content, clear public pages, and real web mentions matter more than AI-only files, prompt-stuffed pages, forced chunking, or fake forum activity.

## Intent To Asset

Map the keyword to the asset searchers expect:

- `Transactional`: landing page, tool page, pricing, booking, checkout, or paid CTA.
- `Commercial`: comparison page, alternatives page, review/proof page, use-case page.
- `Informational`: guide, tutorial, example library, technical explainer, or content page.
- `Tool/generator`: working free tool, demo, calculator, generator, or template.
- `Integration`: "[product] for [platform]" page with setup proof and use case.
- `Navigational`: usually ignore for acquisition unless it exposes brand confusion.

Do not build the asset you prefer. Build the asset the searcher and current search results imply.

## pSEO Pattern Router

Pick the page pattern from the product asset, not keyword volume alone.

- `Database/inventory`: entity, category, local, benchmark, directory, or listing pages backed by real records.
- `Comparison/alternative`: honest competitor, category, and replacement pages for commercial intent.
- `Free tool/calculator/generator`: searched job solved directly with a lightweight working tool.
- `Integration`: real platform/use-case page with setup proof, screenshots, limitations, and CTA.
- `Examples/templates`: product outputs, document examples, prompts, checklists, or templates with paid upgrade path.
- `Local/category`: location/category pages only when each page has unique local data, inventory, or decision value.
- `Glossary/educational hub`: useful for trust-heavy or complex categories, but link toward higher-intent assets.
- `UGC/data loop`: users or customers improve pages over time; do not fake UGC.

If the founder has no unique data, inventory, tool, template, integration, example, or proof, do not recommend pSEO. Route to validation, outbound, community research, or one manually produced asset.

## Match And Exceed

For each target keyword or page:

1. Inspect the current top results.
2. Identify the dominant page type.
3. Note what is weak: outdated examples, shallow content, bad UX, missing tool, weak proof, slow page, no niche specificity, low authority.
4. Decide whether you can exceed it with current constraints.

If you cannot exceed the current results, do not spend founder time there yet. Pick a narrower long-tail keyword, another channel, or a manual validation action.

## Focused Money-Page Sprint

When a product has at least some buyer proof and one high-value query matters, prefer a focused sprint over scattered SEO work.

Use this when:

- one page is already getting impressions but sits outside the top results;
- one keyword clearly maps to the paid offer;
- competitors with weaker product or content outrank the founder;
- the page has a working conversion path.

Sprint actions:

- improve the one page's promise, title, H1, proof, screenshots, CTA, speed, and internal links;
- add 3-10 relevant internal links from existing pages;
- create or improve one supporting free tool, example, comparison, or proof asset only if it strengthens the money page;
- build a small list of specific backlink/mention targets;
- log changes and measure impressions, clicks, qualified conversions, and revenue.

Do not run a sprint on a vague keyword, a page without a paid boundary, or a query where the searcher is not a likely buyer.

## Free Tool Guardrails

A free tool is worth building only when:

- It solves a searched job directly.
- It demonstrates the paid product's value.
- It can earn links or shares because it is useful on its own.
- It has a clear paid boundary, upsell, capture, or next action.
- It can be shipped quickly and maintained cheaply.

Do not build free tools that attract non-buyers, create support burden, or give away the full paid value.

Good free-tool wedges for technical solo founders:

- `Checker`: scans a page, file, domain, feed, profile, or account and reveals one painful gap.
- `Calculator`: estimates cost, traffic value, time saved, risk, backlog size, or ROI.
- `Extractor`: turns messy input into structured output the buyer needs.
- `Generator`: creates a small useful artifact, template, report, image, file, or plan.
- `Inspector`: shows what a system can see, miss, parse, crawl, or understand.
- `Benchmark`: compares the user's current state against competitors, standards, or best practices.

The free tool should create one of three next actions: upgrade for depth/scale, book a paid review/pilot, or capture email for a high-intent follow-up. If it only creates anonymous usage and support, it is probably founder entertainment.

For OpenSEO-style workflows, use the free tool idea as an SEO asset candidate: store the query, SERP shape, expected output, page URL, paid boundary, and measurement plan in the SEO workspace before building.

## pSEO Guardrails

Use pSEO only when:

- There are many real long-tail intents.
- Each page can provide unique value, not just swapped keywords.
- You have a real data source, user-generated content, examples, templates, inventory, or integrations.
- Pages can be internally linked and grouped clearly.
- Indexing can start with a small high-quality batch.

Avoid:
- Thin AI-generated pages.
- Duplicate titles, descriptions, and body content.
- Keyword stuffing.
- Cannibalization: multiple pages targeting the same primary search job.
- Generating thousands of pages before knowing which pages deserve to rank.

AI can assist research, structure, drafts, and transformations. Do not publish generic AI slop without product evidence, human judgment, or unique value.

## Small-Batch pSEO Protocol

Do not scale pSEO from a spreadsheet directly to thousands of URLs.

1. Choose one page pattern and one buyer/search job.
2. Build 5-30 high-quality pages, or one free tool plus 5-10 support pages.
3. Make each page useful without the paid product, but clearly connected to the paid product.
4. Add internal links from hub -> cluster -> related pages -> paid boundary.
5. Measure indexation, impressions, clicks, qualified signup/trial/reply/demo, payment, and retention.
6. Prune, merge, noindex, or rewrite pages that get impressions but no qualified action.
7. Scale only the page type that produces buyer-qualified behavior.

Search impressions prove visibility. Clicks prove curiosity. Qualified conversion proves message-market fit. Payment and retention prove business value.

## Backlink Authority Guardrails

Backlinks amplify a strong page. They do not repair weak product, vague positioning, bad UX, or missing payment path.

Before backlink outreach, check:

- Is the page useful, fast, credible, and conversion-ready?
- Is there a specific reason another site would reference it?
- Does the target site already cover this job, competitor, category, or tool type?
- Would the link help a buyer, not only the founder?

Good backlink work:
- Competitor mention replacement when your product is genuinely better.
- Case studies for tools you use.
- Reporter/source responses when you have credible expertise or data.
- Product-update outreach to writers who already cover the category.
- Broken-link replacement with a genuinely useful asset.
- Affiliate/referral programs after the product converts.

Avoid:
- Cheap backlink packages.
- Spammy directories.
- Forum/comment spam.
- Private blog networks.
- Over-optimized anchor text.
- Irrelevant link swaps.
- Acquired-domain redirects without topical and backlink-history due diligence.

For `$1k+ MRR`, a backlink plan should list 10 specific target pages/sites and why each would rationally link. For pre-revenue, prefer product/demand proof unless the SEO asset itself is the MVP.

## Metrics And Tools

Use tools only to answer the next MRR question:

- OpenSEO workspace: project context, GSC intake, keyword/page notes, competitors, content briefs, outreach notes, and reports.
- Ahrefs/Semrush/Mangools: demand, competition, content gaps, backlinks.
- Google Search Console: queries, impressions, click-through, indexed pages.
- Analytics/PostHog: landing conversion, activation, paid conversion.
- Stripe/payment data: whether SEO traffic pays.

Ask for at most 1-2 missing metrics. Do not create a dashboard project.

If the user has OpenSEO configured, route deeper setup/audit work to the OpenSEO/SEO skills after the business gate is clear:

- `seo-project-setup`: create or refresh the durable SEO workspace, connect the right site/project, and ingest Search Console context.
- `seo-audit`: diagnose crawlability, indexation, technical, on-page, content, and authority issues for the chosen pages.

Keep this skill responsible for deciding whether SEO work should happen now and what MRR question it must answer.

## Search And Ads Validation Metrics

Use keyword and ad tools only to answer the next MRR question.

- `Keyword tools`: search volume, trend, CPC, paid competition, SERP type, and competitor pages.
- `Search Console`: query/page fit, impressions, click-through, indexed pages, and AI-feature reporting if available.
- `Ads`: exact/phrase query, ad promise, landing CTA, conversion event, and cost per qualified action.
- `Analytics`: signup, activation, demo booking, email capture, checkout, paid pilot.
- `Revenue`: payment, repeat payment, retention, expansion.

A small Google Ads test can validate promise/query/CTA fit. It cannot validate SEO moat, retention, or profitable acquisition unless conversion and revenue are tracked.

## Output Add-On

When this reference is relevant, add only these fields to the default output:

- `Search-demand signal`: main query/job, demand strength, and confidence.
- `Intent asset`: the page/tool/content type that fits the query.
- `SEO hard gate`: what blocks SEO work, if anything.
- `Next organic action`: one page/tool/outreach action tied to MRR.
