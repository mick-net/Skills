# Founder Evidence And Who Said What

Use this when the user wants the specific founder origin of a principle. Verify live sources before treating revenue or current bio claims as current.

## Pieter Levels

Evidence:
- `levels.io/projects`: documents the 2014 "12 startups in 12 months" period, Nomad List, Remote OK, and later projects.
- `levels.io/idea-validation`: paid validation framing.
- Lex Fridman transcript: discusses PhotoAI and simple PHP/jQuery/SQLite style operations.

Principles:
- Volume beats perfection when market probes are cheap.
- Charge early; payment is stronger validation than praise.
- Use a simple stack you can ship without thinking.
- Public building and open revenue are distribution when paired with real products.
- Build near a niche identity: digital nomads, remote workers, AI headshots.

Coach translation:
- "Why are you protecting the idea instead of shipping the smallest paid test?"
- "What can be launched this week with a simple stack?"
- "Does this product have a niche identity or only a feature list?"

## Yasser Elsaid

Evidence:
- Supabase customer story: Chatbase MVP speed and early revenue/traffic milestones.
- ProductLed and Solo Founders interviews: bootstrapped ARR, self-serve to sales evolution, pricing/content/outbound lessons.
- Chatbase product/pricing pages: self-serve plus enterprise packaging.

Principles:
- Catch platform shifts early, but move toward a painful business workflow.
- Self-serve onboarding is the early sales team.
- Use product demos and customer stories as content.
- Add sales, warm outbound, and enterprise trust only after PLG demand exists.

Coach translation:
- "Can a stranger reach value in 60 seconds?"
- "Are you building an enterprise fantasy before self-serve users pay?"
- "Where is the customer story?"

## Tony Dinh

Evidence:
- Tony's TypingMind launch post: domain on March 2, MVP on March 6, first sales, $10k revenue by March 10, Product Hunt on March 11.
- Tony's posts on Black Magic: platform API risk.
- TypingMind pricing/docs: BYO-key personal licenses and Teams expansion.

Principles:
- Scratch your own itch inside a market with urgent timing.
- Keep products low-maintenance: static, local, desktop, BYO-key where possible.
- Use one-time pricing only when marginal costs are near zero.
- Turn every meaningful feature into a marketing post.
- Move from personal license to B2B only when teams naturally pull it.

Coach translation:
- "Does this architecture lower maintenance or create a support treadmill?"
- "Can this feature announcement sell the product today?"
- "Are recurring costs compatible with lifetime pricing?"

## Jon Yongfook

Evidence:
- Bannerbear "Journey to $10K MRR": seven unrevenue-generating products taught shipping, then focus on image generation; API pivot; one week code / one week marketing; JTBD repositioning around automating/scaling marketing.
- Bannerbear "1 Year of Marketing a SaaS from $10K MRR to $27K MRR": marketing is growth work that is not building features; technical content, tutorials, demos, docs, community, open startup page, free tools.
- Bannerbear "7 Lessons Growing a Bootstrapped SaaS to $50K MRR": product-founder fit, do not price too low, repeat what works, create free tools, be retweet-able.

Principles:
- Technical founders must schedule marketing, not wait to feel like marketers.
- A 50/50 code/marketing rhythm can be more valuable than another feature sprint.
- Documentation, tutorials, demos, and free tools are marketing assets.
- Niche down the target market, not the pain until it is too small to matter.
- Jobs-to-be-done language usually appears after talking to users and watching usage.

Coach translation:
- "Your git log shows code. Where is the matching marketing week?"
- "What documentation, tutorial, demo, or free tool did this feature create?"
- "Do you know the job-to-be-done, or are you still describing implementation?"

## Arvid Kahl / FeedbackPanda

Evidence:
- Indie Hackers coverage: FeedbackPanda reached $55k MRR and sold; audience research was central; target users were freelance English teachers in Facebook groups; product saved about two hours of unpaid daily work.
- Arvid's broader work: Zero to Sold and Embedded Entrepreneur emphasize embedded audience research.

Principles:
- Specific communities beat abstract personas.
- The best SaaS wedge often removes repetitive unpaid work.
- Audience research comes before product certainty.
- Calm, sellable, sustainable growth can be a better target than hype.

Coach translation:
- "Where do your buyers already complain together?"
- "What unpaid repeated work are you eliminating?"
- "Can you name the community, not just the ICP?"

## Danny Postma

Evidence:
- Indie Hackers coverage of Headlime: AI writing tool sold for roughly seven figures after fast growth.
- Founder-story coverage of HeadshotPro: AI headshots as a strong platform-timing / SEO / paid-output wedge. Treat current revenue claims as self-reported unless verified.

Principles:
- Ride platform shifts with a clear paid output, not vague AI capability.
- Use SEO and simple landing pages around high-intent use cases.
- Paid outputs such as headshots, generated assets, or done-for-you deliverables can validate faster than complex SaaS workflows.

Coach translation:
- "What is the paid output?"
- "Can the buyer understand the before/after without a demo call?"
- "Is this an AI capability or a productized result?"

## Cross-Founder Synthesis

- Pieter: ship cheap tests, charge early, keep stack simple.
- Yasser: compress self-serve activation and move toward budget-owning workflows.
- Marc: make proof and launch storytelling part of distribution.
- Tony: reduce operational drag and market every feature.
- Jon: enforce a code/marketing rhythm and use docs/demos/free tools as growth.
- Damon: talk to customers daily and build proof products.
- Arvid: embed in a specific community and remove repeated unpaid work.
- Danny: sell a clear output from a platform shift.

The harsh version:
If the founder cannot name the buyer, show the paid path, point to customer notes, and show one marketing asset from this week, the problem is not the codebase.
