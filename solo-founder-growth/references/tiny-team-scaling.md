# Tiny-Team Scaling

Use this when a bootstrapped product is no longer only validation-constrained. The goal is not to make a solo founder act like a VC-backed company; it is to preserve leverage while handling real operating load.

## Use When

- Support, onboarding, implementation, reliability, procurement, regulatory, security, or compliance load appears.
- The founder is the bottleneck for customer success, incident response, sales follow-up, or delivery.
- Revenue is growing but margin, support burden, customer concentration, or service promises make the business fragile.
- The user asks whether to stay solo, hire, outsource, automate, add process, or go upmarket.

## Do Not Use When

- The product is still prelaunch or launched with no meaningful buyer proof.
- The problem is still unclear buyer, weak public promise, missing paid boundary, or no reachable channel.
- The founder is asking for scale process to avoid sales, support, pricing, or customer conversations.

## Operating Load Override

MRR routes growth advice; it does not determine staffing. Check these dimensions independently:

- `Purchase friction`: public pricing and card purchase vs security review, legal redlines, procurement, vendor registration, or multi-party approval.
- `Delivery burden`: standard onboarding vs migration, training, custom integrations, implementation, or change management.
- `Trust/regulation`: low-sensitivity reversible work vs regulated data, audit obligations, formal controls, or contractual risk ownership.
- `Support/reliability`: best-effort async support vs response-time promises, on-call, 24/7 expectations, or mission-critical workflows.
- `Economics/continuity`: high margin and diversified revenue vs high variable cost, customer concentration, abuse/fraud exposure, or founder as single point of failure.

If any dimension creates material customer harm, legal exposure, or revenue fragility, add process or help even when MRR is low. If all dimensions are low-load, stay solo/tiny longer.

## Solo, Process, Contractor, Or Hire

- Stay solo/tiny when the work is low-risk, low-load, self-serve, documented, and not blocking revenue or customer trust.
- Add process when the same mistake, question, support issue, onboarding step, or operational task repeats.
- Use a contractor when the work is bounded, specifiable, and not core product judgment.
- Hire when a recurring function needs durable ownership, customer context, availability, or continuous improvement.
- Do not hire because MRR crossed a vanity milestone. Hire against a named queue, owner gap, failure cost, and expected outcome.

## Scaling Principles

1. Low-touch scale must be engineered: pricing, onboarding, docs, support tooling, purchasing, and product UX are one system.
2. Productize repeated support before adding support capacity: repeated ticket -> root-cause fix, docs, guardrail, or automation.
3. Standardize work before scaling headcount: owner, queue, definition of done, runbook, cadence.
4. Preserve direct customer contact after delegation: delegate queue handling, not customer understanding.
5. Scale one compounding distribution surface before adding channels: search, integrations, referrals, shareable output, audience, or ecosystem.
6. Let upmarket demand create a hybrid motion: add sales only when account economics and procurement justify it.
7. Price for the service promise: support, reliability, compliance, onboarding, and variable costs must be funded.
8. Watch continuity, not only MRR: founder hours, concentration, incidents, cash coverage, and margin can break a growing business.
9. Treat compliance as feasibility first: identify deal blockers early, but do full certification only after qualified demand or obligation.
10. Keep scope small even when scaling: systematize what works before expanding product surface, market, or org structure.

## Productized-Service Boundary

Use this when a solo founder adds services, paid pilots, audits, implementation, or concierge delivery on top of software.

- The factory is now part of the product: onboarding, inputs, reports, customer communication, quality review, refund policy, and delivery cadence need explicit ownership.
- Document before automating. A screenshot-level runbook is often cheaper than brittle automation until the workflow repeats.
- Kill options that do not improve results: fewer languages, channels, targets, meetings, or formats can improve quality and reduce load.
- Do not sell unlimited access to compensate for weak results. More calls, custom work, and vague "we will help with everything" promises usually raise expectations and support load.
- Delegate execution without losing judgment. Keep a recurring founder review of customer outcomes, objections, churn reasons, and delivery quality.
- Hire or contract only against a named bottleneck: repetitive operations, client communication, specialist delivery, sales follow-up, or edge-case handling.
- Allow some inefficiency when it protects continuity, quality, and founder energy. The target is durable margin and customer trust, not theoretical maximum efficiency.

## Minimal Scale Metrics

Pick at most one or two metrics that change the next decision:

- Contribution or gross margin.
- Cash coverage.
- Founder hours by function.
- Tickets per active account.
- Time to first value and onboarding failure rate.
- Revenue concentration by customer or segment.
- Net revenue retention or expansion.
- Incidents, SLA misses, failed jobs, or manual interventions.
- Sales cycle length and procurement/security-review frequency.

## AI Delegation Boundary

Agents can help preserve a tiny team by preparing, monitoring, summarizing, and executing reversible work:

- Classify support tickets, summarize themes, draft replies and help articles.
- Research accounts, enrich CRM notes, draft outreach and follow-ups.
- Draft comparison pages, docs, release notes, onboarding emails, demos, and content briefs.
- Build funnel summaries, cohort notes, anomaly reports, and churn summaries.
- Generate tests, triage logs, draft runbooks, reproduce bugs, and monitor failed jobs.
- Gather compliance evidence from approved docs and draft questionnaire responses.

Keep these human/founder-owned:

- Discovery calls, pricing judgment, negotiation, and strategic account promises.
- Positioning, factual claims, final publication approval, and public brand voice.
- Legal representations, audit attestations, policy ownership, and customer sign-off.
- Production approval for destructive changes, security risk acceptance, and incident command.
- Hiring, firing, compensation, feedback, and culture decisions.

Rule: automate preparation, reversible execution, monitoring, and queue reduction. Require human approval for external promises, privileged access, irreversible changes, money movement, legal assertions, personnel decisions, and material incidents.

## Stage Adaptations

`$1k-$10k MRR`: stay focused on retention and repeatability. Add lightweight tracking for founder hours, support themes, margin, and customer concentration only when they affect the next move.

`$10k-$100k MRR`: systematize proven acquisition and delivery. Remove founder bottlenecks with docs, automation, contractors, or a narrow hire. Add the minimum trust/procurement path required by qualified accounts.

`$100k+ MRR`: improve owner independence, service quality, unit economics, risk coverage, and segment-specific sales/support. Avoid headcount and management layers as status symbols.

## Do Not Copy

- Headcount benchmarks tied to MRR.
- VC org charts, executive layers, OKR theater, or board-style planning.
- Full SOC 2, ISO, HIPAA, GDPR, PCI, procurement, or enterprise-sales playbooks before real pull.
- Founder biographies, revenue-per-employee comparisons, or “how they scaled” storytelling.
- More channels, more products, or more people before the existing engine is repeatable.

## Evidence Notes

- Atlassian shows that low-touch/self-serve scale is an integrated operating model, not just “no salespeople”: transparent pricing, online purchase, easy adoption, docs, support, and automation.
- 37signals/Basecamp shows the value of bounded appetite, small teams, and variable scope when work starts slowing down.
- Zapier shows customer-support exposure can feed product, docs, and internal tooling, but it is mostly seed-strapped rather than purely bootstrapped.
- ConvertKit/Kit shows rapid bootstrapped growth can create cash, support, infrastructure, abuse, and staffing constraints at the same time.
- Ahrefs shows one product-linked distribution engine can scale far when content, product, and search intent reinforce each other.
- Balsamiq shows visual virality and customer love can scale, but support overload and coordination eventually need ownership and process.
- Mailchimp shows bootstrapped companies can become very large by serving SMBs with a focused low-touch product, but that scale should not be copied as an early-stage operating model.
